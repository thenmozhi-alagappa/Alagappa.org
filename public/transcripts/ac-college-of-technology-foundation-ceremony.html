Speeches of Alagappa Chettiar

Speech of Dr. RM. Alagappa Chettiar to Welcome Dr. Rajendra Prasad, President of India, Requesting Him to Lay the Foundation Stone of the Alagappa Chettiar College of Engineering and Technology at Karaikudi

On 19th February 1953

Respected Sir,

It is my privilege and pleasure to welcome you here on behalf of the various institutions under the Trust – the Alagappa Chettiar College of Engineering and Technology, the Arts & Science College, the Training College and the Model High School. The Arts and Science College was started in 1947 and has been developing in strength, in commemoration of the centenary of Dr. Besant, who has done so much for the resurgence of nationalism in this country. The college was also housed, in the beginning, in the "Gandhi Maligai", thus constantly bringing to our minds the great leaders to whom we owe our freedom and our moral renaissance. The idea of starting a College of Engineering and Technology as part of the various educational institutions under the Trust took shape as early as 1948, when Prime Minister Pandit Nehru came here to lay the foundation stone of the Central Electrochemical Research Institute, which was declared open by the Vice-President, Dr. Radhakrishnan, on 14th January, 1953. The College actually started work in July of last year. It is housed temporarily in the premises of the Arts & Science College, making use of the science laboratories, workshop and common drawing hall belonging to it. A first batch of 73 students has been selected. It is hardly necessary to add that a technological college cannot function except in its own buildings suited to its needs, and therefore the Trustees have been extremely anxious to construct an independent set of buildings for it with the least possible delay. In this connection our gratitude is due to the Government of Madras for having readily placed at our disposal a sum of Rs. 4 lakhs as an initial half-grant towards equipment and buildings for the current year. It is indeed our good fortune, and, may I add, a good omen – and, from the traditional religious point of view, nothing could be more auspicious than that you should be performing this act immediately after worshipping at the sacred temple of Rameswaram, where Lord Rama himself, prior to his conquest of Lanka, installed and worshipped Lord Maheswara.

It is our ambition to create within this campus a centre of learning and culture in all its aspects. There are at present located here an Arts & Science College with nearly a thousand students, a Training College training about 70 teachers of post-graduate standard, and the new Engineering College. The Central Electrochemical Research Institute – one of the eleven National Laboratories of India – will be a centre of higher research in a field of great significance, both in knowledge as such and in the industrial field. It is also proposed to shift the Ramanujam Institute of Mathematics, which I was prevailed upon to start three years ago, to Karaikudi. The students and staff of these institutions form a colony in which learning is, as it were, part of the very atmosphere, so that, gradually, what is created is not merely a place of learning but a way of life. It is this higher objective of inculcating curiosity as a life pursuit and acceptance of truth at all costs that should be the aim of all academies. It is our hope that such a fulfilment will be facilitated by the creation of a number of institutions on this campus, each pursuing its own line of study but mingling with the others. It is with this view that we also hope to establish, before long, living accommodation for the students and staff within the campus itself to the maximum extent possible; for otherwise the busy hours of collegiate instruction and work are not sufficient to enable the students and staff to come into intimate contact, which alone will help to create in the younger mind a proper attitude towards learning and life. It is our ambition to enlarge the scope of the instruction given here by such additions as other branches of engineering, polytechnics, technology and the like.

It is hardly necessary at this stage in the development of our country to stress the need for sufficient technical personnel, especially on the engineering side. It has been remarked by foreign observers, not in a spirit of criticism but in all fairness, that the Indian student is better in theory than in the practice of a technique. This is perhaps due to a psychological inheritance on our part, which makes us feel that skill in the use of the hands is a subordinate attainment. Modern technical efficiency, however, can never be attained or respected in full measure unless the hand and the head are in perfect co-ordination. In this respect, engineering is like painting and sculpture. It is our hope that we shall be able to correct this defect.

At the same time it is not our aim to look back rather than forward, and to glorify the past, or to be weighed down by tradition. The culture of this country was great only when it was dynamic and fearless, and even in the spiritual field asserted change when it was felt to be necessary. Otherwise, Hinduism and Buddhism could not have grown and lived side by side, nor, reverting to the field of architecture, could a happy blending have been effected between the style of the Hindus and the style of the Moghuls. It is this tradition of intelligent compromise and change that we should aim at in all things, and we hope that those whom we train in our institutions, with your blessings, Sir, will develop that type of mind which will get the best of both worlds.

Let me conclude by saying that we attach the deepest significance to your laying the foundation stone of this College. Apart from your position as President of the Indian Union, you represent in your own individual right all that is best in the culture of our land. You are one of that selected band of great men who gathered round Mahatma Gandhi from the earliest days of our struggle for freedom, by way of Truth and Non-violence. You have emerged from the struggle with victory but not rancour, with calmness and not exuberance, with humility and not pride. This is indeed true to the tradition of our great land. It is our devout prayer that it will be given to us to benefit by your example, and that your visit will not merely be a happy memory but a permanent guidance.

I thank you for having been in our midst on this occasion, and request you, Sir, to lay the foundation stone of the buildings of the Alagappa Chettiar College of Engineering and Technology, of the Dr. Alagappa Chettiar College Endowment Trust.
